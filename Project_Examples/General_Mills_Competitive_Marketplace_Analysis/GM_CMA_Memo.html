RE: General Mills Competitive Marketplace Analysis

In this analysis I examine General Mills cereal product and strategy performance relative to our two main competitors: Kelloggs and Post. This data consists of the sales records for 15 different cereals across 20,000 stores abd three brands. It includes the use of promotions and advertisements, weekly sales totals, store information, and the sale price. This sales information is important because these brands are our competitors and consumers are likely looking at all three brands products simultaneously when making purchasing decisions. In this analyses Ive come up with two interesting findings. 1) General Mills outperforms both Kelloggs and Post in average weekly revenue in most marketing scenarios. And 2) Cocoa Puffs specially performs best with a promotion accompanied by a small advertisement. I will go further into detail with these monetarly.

Fig 1

As we can see in this figure on the right, General Mills is reliably returning more average revenue per week in almost all scenarios. The highest performing scenario is the combination of promotions and medium advertisements. This is followed closely by promotions without advertisements and both are significantly greater than weeks with no marketing. Weeks with promotions or advertisements (medium or small) all do relatively equal in terms of average revenue. The only situation in which General Mills is beat by the competition is when Kelloggs is running promotions with a small advertisement. Otherwise General Mills leads Post and Kelloggs consistently.

Fig 2

When it comes to specific cereal performance, Cocoa Puffs is a lowest performer. Here we can see the average weekly revenue is about $18. With the use of promotions and ads we see an increase of $14.50 to an average of $32.50 weekly. This is a significant jump over weeks with no marketing and the product being sold at MSRP. The highest revenue scenario, as you can see, is the promotion coupled with a medium advertisement. Interestingly, weeks where there is no promotion but only ads, either small or medium in size, result in lower weekly revenue than if no marketing were to take place at all.

In conclusion I recommend two courses of action: The first is increasing the number of promotions combined with small advertisement across the product line. Depending on advertisement costs, it may be more cost effective to forgo advertisements and instead focus on promotions. The second is specifically increasing the amount of small advertisements combined with promotions for Cocoa Puffs. General Mills marketing and promotions are competitive, but focusing on these specific strategies can likely boost weekly revenue.
